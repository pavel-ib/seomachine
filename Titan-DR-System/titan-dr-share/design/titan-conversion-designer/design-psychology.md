# DESIGN PSYCHOLOGY — Visual Conversion Principles

> The visual equivalent of titan-psychology.md.
> These are not aesthetic guidelines. They are behavioral engineering tools.

---

## 1. VISUAL CORTEX PROCESSING ORDER

The eye doesn't scan randomly. It follows a hard-wired sequence:

**Processing order (fastest to slowest):**
1. **Motion** — anything moving gets processed before anything static (video > static always at TOF)
2. **Faces** — the brain has dedicated face-detection hardware (fusiform face area). A human face registers before any text or product.
3. **High contrast edges** — bright-on-dark or dark-on-bright areas snap attention
4. **Color** — warm colors (red, orange, yellow) register before cool colors (blue, green)
5. **Size** — larger elements register before smaller ones
6. **Text** — words are processed last, after the visual scene is assessed

**Conversion implication:** Your most important message should be carried by the element that processes fastest — a face's expression, a color contrast, a large number — not buried in a headline that processes 200ms later.

## 2. COLOR PSYCHOLOGY FOR DTC CTR

Use color as a signal system, not a brand preference:

| Color | Psychological Signal | DTC Use Case | Warning |
|-------|---------------------|--------------|---------|
| **Red** | Urgency, danger, stop, importance | Scarcity/sale callouts, countdown elements, "stop scrolling" flash | Overuse = visual noise. Max 1 red element per ad. |
| **Orange** | Energy, action, warmth, accessible | CTA buttons (highest CTR color for CTAs in most DTC verticals), safety/emergency, sports nutrition | Can feel cheap at premium price points |
| **Blue** | Trust, authority, calm, medical | Doctor/clinical ads, financial products, dental, skincare | Cold. Use warm variant (#2563EB, not #001F8A) for DTC. |
| **Green** | Health, natural, safety, permission | First aid products, safety gear, "safe to try" framing | Overused in health/safety. Differentiation opportunity to NOT use it. |
| **Black** | Premium, authority, confidence | Luxury positioning, dark mode premium feels, high-ticket | Low CTR on Facebook (thumb-stopping requires contrast with feed's white) |
| **White** | Clean, clinical, minimal, medical | Science/ingredient ads, clinical proof, premium minimal | Invisible against Facebook's white feed. Must pair with strong color accent. |
| **Yellow/Gold** | Attention, value, warmth | Star ratings, "best value" badges, limited-time callouts | Unreadable as text background |
| **Purple** | Luxury, anti-aging, femininity, mystery | Skincare, premium wellness, sleep, anti-aging | Niche — strong for right category |

**The DTC CTR Color Stack (ranked by historical CTR):**
1. Orange CTA button on dark background — highest CTR for DTC product offers
2. Red + white — scarcity + urgency (flash sale, countdown)
3. Green + white — health trust signal for natural products
4. Dark navy + orange — tactical premium positioning (SurviveX pattern)

## 3. CONTRAST AND PATTERN INTERRUPT

**The scroll-stop problem:** The Facebook/Instagram feed is a stream of content competing for the same 250ms of attention. Your ad has one job: break the pattern.

**Pattern interrupt principles:**

- **Color contrast vs. feed:** Facebook's feed is predominantly blue (#1877F2), white (#FFFFFF), and grey. Dark backgrounds (black, deep navy) create instant contrast. Bright warm colors (orange, red, coral) break the cool-blue pattern.
- **Composition contrast:** If most ads show products on white, show yours on black. If most show lifestyle scenes, show a stark close-up. The pattern break IS the scroll stop.
- **Visual surprise:** Something that doesn't make immediate sense forces a second look — but must resolve within 2 seconds or the thumb scrolls on. Obscured reveals, unusual angles, unexpected juxtapositions.
- **Text size contrast:** Large, bold, single word or number ("SOLD OUT", "219") forces processing before the viewer decides to scroll.

**High-performing pattern interrupts from the library:**
- Dark background when everyone else is white (SurviveX tactical dark mode)
- Real face in emotional distress (problem-state, not aspirational) — breaks the aspirational lifestyle pattern
- Illustrated/cartoon in a feed of photos (emergency scenario illustration)
- All-text card with no product image (preparedness wake-up call)

## 4. F-PATTERN AND Z-PATTERN READING LAYOUTS

**F-Pattern** (for text-heavy ads / landing page headers):
- Eye scans left-to-right across the TOP
- Then left-to-right again, slightly below, but shorter
- Then drops down the LEFT EDGE vertically
- Result: Top-left is seen by 100% of viewers. Bottom-right is seen by ~30%.

**Z-Pattern** (for visually sparse layouts — most DTC ads):
- Eye starts TOP-LEFT
- Moves to TOP-RIGHT
- Diagonal sweep to BOTTOM-LEFT
- Ends at BOTTOM-RIGHT (the natural CTA placement zone)

**DTC ad placement rules:**
- Brand logo → top-left (seen first, low conversion priority) OR omit entirely for TOF ads
- Most important message → top-left or top-center
- Product → center or center-right (Z diagonal intercept)
- CTA button / price → bottom-right (Z endpoint = action zone)
- Social proof (stars, number) → directly above or below the CTA to catch the eye at the action moment

## 5. FACE DIRECTION PSYCHOLOGY

Faces in ads direct viewer attention. This is not a metaphor — eye-tracking studies show viewers look where pictured faces look.

**Rules:**
- **Face looking at camera:** Creates connection, eye contact, trust. Best for testimonials, spokesperson ads, UGC. The face demands the viewer meet its gaze.
- **Face looking at product:** Directs viewer's gaze to the product. Use when product needs visual emphasis. The viewer follows the model's eye line.
- **Face looking at headline:** Directs attention to copy. Use for complex product explanations or when the mechanism matters.
- **Face looking off-screen:** Creates curiosity/unease. Can be a scroll-stopper but reduces trust. Avoid unless the discomfort is intentional.

**Age/gender matching rule:** The face should represent the avatar, not aspirational. Problem-aware L2 ads: show the CURRENT state (older, tired, problem visible). Transformation ads: show the AFTER state (younger-looking, energized, solved). The before-face and after-face serve different conversion jobs.

**Face vs. no face:**
- Ads with faces: Higher initial engagement (thumb-stop rate), higher CTR on emotional hooks
- Ads without faces: Lower thumb-stop but cleaner product focus. Better for mechanism/feature ads (L3) and offer ads (L5)

## 6. SOCIAL PROOF VISUAL DESIGN

Social proof in visuals works differently than social proof in copy. The visual registers first.

**Visual social proof hierarchy (most to least powerful):**

1. **Real people in before/after** — specific, named, before + after photos side by side. The most credible visual format. Also the most regulated — must be real, documented.
2. **User-generated content style** — low production quality, natural lighting, "selfie aesthetic." The imperfection signals authenticity. High trust, high CTR for warm audiences.
3. **Star rating + number** — "⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 18,447 reviews" in large font. Processed quickly. Best paired with a face or product image.
4. **Testimonial billboard** — screenshot of review, or single quote in large text. Works best for specific outcomes ("Lost 22 lbs in 3 months").
5. **Media logos** — "As seen in..." with recognizable logos. Trust by association. More effective for colder audiences.
6. **Volume claims** — "200,000+ kits sold", "Trusted by 50,000+ families". Volume = safety. The herd instinct.

**Placement rules:**
- Star rating → above the product image (sets trust before the product is evaluated)
- Review quote → below the product or in the ad body (after initial interest established)
- Before/after → split vertically, before on LEFT (discomfort), after on RIGHT (resolution). The eye reads left to right, so this matches the emotional journey.
- Volume number → large, centered or top — use high contrast to make it stop-scroll worthy

## 7. MOBILE-FIRST DESIGN CONSTRAINTS

90%+ of DTC ad impressions are mobile. Design for the smallest screen first.

**Mobile specs (Facebook/Instagram feed):**
- Safe zone: 1:1 square (1080×1080px) — renders without cropping in all placements
- Story / Reels: 9:16 vertical (1080×1920px)
- Text coverage: Facebook penalizes text covering >20% of image. Keep text to headline + CTA only.
- Thumb-stop zone: First 300ms. The top 40% of the image is what renders before the thumb completes the scroll decision.

**Mobile typography minimums:**
- Headline: ≥ 36pt / 48px — readable without zooming on 375px-wide screen
- Body: ≥ 24pt / 32px
- CTA button: ≥ 44pt / 60px tall (touch target minimum)

**Single message rule:** Mobile ads must communicate ONE thing in one glance. If the ad requires reading to understand, it fails on mobile at TOF. The image alone must imply the story.

## 8. BEFORE/AFTER POSITIONING PSYCHOLOGY

The before/after format is one of the highest-converting visual patterns for preparedness and safety DTC. It works because it provides concrete evidence + completes the reader's desire for resolution.

**Execution rules:**
- Before → LEFT side always (problem = past = left in Western reading direction)
- After → RIGHT side (solution = future = right)
- Label them explicitly: "BEFORE" / "AFTER" in clear type — don't make the reader guess
- Include time context: "Before 90 Days / After 90 Days" — gives the transformation a concrete timeline
- Emotional state matching: Before face = tired, frustrated, uncomfortable. After face = energized, confident, lighter.
- The AFTER state should be achievable-looking, not aspirational fantasy — too much transformation reduces credibility

**Regulatory note:** Before/after photos require documentation (timestamped, same conditions, same person). Illustrated or simulated before/after (diagrams, icons, typography contrast) can show the same psychology without the regulatory risk.

## 9. VISUAL HIERARCHY CHECKLIST

Before any design brief is complete, run this check:

- [ ] What does the eye hit FIRST? (Should be the hook/stop element)
- [ ] What does the eye hit SECOND? (Should be the claim/desire)
- [ ] What does the eye hit THIRD? (Should be the product)
- [ ] Is the CTA visually distinct from everything else? (Different color, largest text, clear shape)
- [ ] Does the headline read in 2 seconds on mobile without zooming?
- [ ] Is the background creating contrast with Facebook's white/blue feed?
- [ ] Does the face (if present) direct gaze toward the key message?
- [ ] Is there one dominant element — or is the image "noisy" with competing visual weight?

## 10. PLATFORM-SPECIFIC PATTERN NOTES

**Facebook Feed:**
- Square (1:1) is most versatile — auto-crops to multiple placements
- Dark backgrounds punch through the white feed
- Text overlay limit: keep under 20% of image area
- Best thumb-stop: dramatic contrast in top 40% of image

**Instagram Feed:**
- Slightly more aesthetic-conscious audience — brand consistency matters more
- Flat lay product shots perform well for beauty/skincare
- Carousel ads: First image must stop the thumb, rest can tell the story

**Instagram Stories / Reels:**
- 9:16 vertical only
- Text must be in the CENTER safe zone (top and bottom 15% may be obscured by UI)
- Motion adds disproportionate value here — static story ads perform 30-50% lower

**TikTok:**
- 9:16 only, motion required
- Native aesthetic (lower production = higher trust)
- Sound-on assumptions — unlike Facebook (mostly muted)
