> **Every sentence does at least two jobs.**

> A sentence that does only one job is half a sentence.

## PART 1: MASTER HEADLINE SYSTEM

The headline is the most important element in any copy. Its only job: get the first sentence read.

### The Core Formula: I = B + C
**Interest = Benefit + Curiosity**
Every headline must deliver BOTH:
- Benefit alone: "Be Prepared for Emergencies" — ignorable
- Curiosity alone: "You Won't Believe This" — clickbait
- Combined: "The EMT-Designed System That Turns Everyday People into First Responders" — clicks

---

### The Master Headline Formula

Every headline in direct response — ads, listicles, advertorials, VSLs, landing pages, emails — is built from this architecture:

```
[ENTRY DEVICE] + [SUBJECT] + [TENSION ELEMENT] + [QUALIFIER]
```

| Slot | What It Is | Options |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **ENTRY DEVICE** | How you enter the reader's attention | How To, Why, The [Adjective], [Number] [Frame], Warning:, Announcing:, They Laughed When, At Last, Stop, Give Me, Do You, What, Are You, For the, Breakthrough, NEW |
| **SUBJECT** | What the headline is about — the actor or topic | [Person Type], [Product/Mechanism], [Authority], [Your Current Solution], [Common Advice], [Unlikely Hero], This [Product Descriptor], [Brand], [Demographic] |
| **TENSION ELEMENT** | The verb or state that creates pull — curiosity, fear, desire, or contrast | That [Big Promise], Is [Negative Outcome], Without [Pain], In [Timeframe], While Others [Fail], Discovered [Secret], Makes [Problem] Worse, Reveals [Hidden Truth], Is Trending, Are Switching, Is Crushing |
| **QUALIFIER** | Narrows the audience or adds specificity. Optional but powerful | In [Year], After [Age], For [Demographic], If You [Condition], Until You [Action], (Number [X] Will Shock You), Before It's Too Late, And [Big Promise] |

**Generation process:**
1. Pick an ENTRY DEVICE based on Awareness Level (see routing table below)
2. Fill the SUBJECT with the most relevant actor for your angle
3. Choose a TENSION ELEMENT that creates the strongest pull for your sophistication stage
4. Add a QUALIFIER if it sharpens the audience without limiting reach

---

### Entry Device × Awareness Level Routing

| Awareness Level | Best Entry Devices | Why |
|----------------|-------------------|-----|
| Most Aware | Announcing, NEW, At Last, [Product Name] | They know the product — lead with it |
| Product-Aware | Why [Number], The [Adjective], [Number] [Frame] | They need convincing — lead with proof/superiority |
| Solution-Aware | How To, [Number] Ways, The [Mechanism], Stop, Breakthrough | They know solutions exist — lead with yours being different |
| Problem-Aware | Why, Do You, [Number] Mistakes, Warning | They feel the pain — name it and offer the path |
| Unaware | They Laughed When, Story entry, Are You, What [Authority] | They don't know they have a problem — enter through story/identity |

### The [Number] [Frame] Sub-System

The FRAME slot determines the entire headline angle. Same product, radically different positioning:

| Frame | Emotional Driver | Best For |
|-------|-----------------|----------|
| Reasons Why | Rational justification + curiosity | Listicle page titles, comparison angles |
| Warning Signs | Fear + self-diagnosis | Problem-aware audiences, health |
| Mistakes | Shame avoidance + correction | Education angles, "you're doing it wrong" |
| Secrets | Forbidden knowledge + exclusivity | Suppressed info angles, authority positioning |
| Things | Neutral curiosity + discovery | Broad appeal, low-agitation |
| Signs | Self-identification + concern | Symptom-based, qualifying audiences |
| Ways | Aspiration + variety | Solution-aware, positive framing |
| Lies | Betrayal + anger | Contrarian angles, industry attacks |
| Facts | Authority + undeniability | Data-driven audiences, biohackers |
| Rules | Authority + structure | Prescriptive angles, expert positioning |

**Same product, 10 different frames — all from one formula:**
- **Reasons Why:** "10 Reasons Why 200,000+ Families Upgraded Their First Aid Kit in 2026"
- **Warning Signs:** "7 Warning Signs Your First Aid Kit Will Fail in a Real Emergency"
- **Mistakes:** "5 First Aid Kit Mistakes That Leave Families Dangerously Unprepared (And What to Do Instead)"
- **Secrets:** "The 3 Secrets Your Local Fire Department Knows About Emergency Preparedness That You Don't"
- **Things:** "8 Things That Happen When You Replace Your Dollar-Store Kit with an EMT-Curated System"
- **Signs:** "6 Signs Your Family Needs a Better First Aid Kit (Especially If You Have Kids)"
- **Ways:** "10 Ways This Color-Coded Kit Is Replacing the Entire Medicine Cabinet for Emergencies"
- **Lies:** "5 Lies the First Aid Industry Told You About Being Prepared"
- **Facts:** "9 Facts About Professional First Aid Kits That Cheap Kit Makers Don't Want You to Know"
- **Rules:** "The 7 Rules of Emergency Preparedness That Changed Everything for 200,000+ Families"

### Schwartz Headline Strengtheners (13 Techniques)

After writing a headline, run it through these techniques to increase pulling power. Pick 1-3 that create the most tension for your specific audience.

**STRENGTHENER 1: Measure the Claim (Dimensionalize)**
Add specific numbers. "Be Prepared for Emergencies" → "250 Components Organized into 4 Color-Coded Compartments." Numbers create belief through specificity.

**STRENGTHENER 2: Measure the Speed (Accelerate)**
Add a timeframe. "Better Preparedness" → "Fully Equipped by Tomorrow." Speed creates urgency and believability.

**STRENGTHENER 3: Compare (Antagonize)**
Measure against a known standard. "Complete First Aid Kit" → "3x More Components Than the Leading Competitor at Half the Price Per Item." Comparison creates instant frame of reference.

**STRENGTHENER 4: Metaphorize**
Replace literal description with vivid image. "Organized first aid supplies" → "Every compartment labeled like an EMT's jump bag — your hands go where they need to go before your brain catches up." Metaphors bypass the neocortex and land in the limbic system.

**STRENGTHENER 5: Sensitize**
Add sensory detail that makes the benefit FELT, not just understood. "Better preparedness" → "That quiet confidence when you glance at the kit on the wall and know — whatever happens tonight, you're ready."

**STRENGTHENER 6: Dramatize the Result**
Show the CONSEQUENCE of the benefit, not just the benefit. "Better prepared" → "The kind of prepared where your neighbor calls YOU first when their kid falls off the trampoline." Go two levels deep.

**STRENGTHENER 7: State as Paradox**
Create tension through contradiction. "The First Aid Kit Designed to Be Used by People with Zero Training" or "Why Having FEWER Choices in an Emergency Saves More Lives." Paradox creates irresistible curiosity.

**STRENGTHENER 8: Remove Limitations (Exhort)**
Eliminate the expected restriction. "Be Prepared for Emergencies" → "Be Prepared Without Medical Training, Without Assembling Your Own Kit, Without Spending Hours Researching What to Include."

**STRENGTHENER 9: Challenge the Reader**
Dare them. "Are You Tough Enough to Try This for 30 Days?" Challenge triggers ego involvement.

**STRENGTHENER 10: Connect Mechanism to Claim**
Show the causal link. "The Color-Coded Compartment System That Eliminates Panic in an Emergency." Mechanism + claim in one line = believability + desire.

**STRENGTHENER 11: Mechanize the Benefit**
Name the mechanism behind the result. "Be Prepared" → "Find Exactly What You Need in Under 10 Seconds With Color-Coded Triage Compartments." The mechanism becomes the promise.

**STRENGTHENER 12: Dimensionalize Across Multiple Claims**
Present the same claim from multiple angles. "Prepared at Home, Protected on the Road, Confident in the Wild." Each dimension hits a different desire pathway.

**STRENGTHENER 13: Reverse the Frame**
Instead of "get the benefit," flip to "eliminate the obstacle." "Better Prepared" → "Eliminate the Panic Gap Between Emergency and Response."

**APPLICATION RULE:** Write the raw headline first. Then run through strengtheners and pick 1-3 that create maximum tension. Metaphor + Speed + Compare will almost always outperform no strengtheners.

## PART 1B: SCHWARTZ'S 7 BODY COPY TECHNIQUES

These are the mechanical processes for shaping the three dimensions of the prospect's mind (Desires / Identifications / Beliefs) at the sentence-by-sentence level. Apply these inside any format. Works in tandem with the Strengtheners.

### Technique 1: Intensification (Strengthening Desire)
Vague desires don't motivate action. You must fill out vague desire with concrete, vivid images of fulfillment. 13 ways to intensify:

1. Put the product IN ACTION — show it working, not sitting on a shelf
2. Put the READER in the middle of the action — they are using it, feeling it
3. Stretch benefits out over time — Day 1 → Day 7 → Day 30 → Day 90
4. Bring in an AUDIENCE that admires the reader for their result
5. Show CONTRAST — old painful way vs. new effortless way (before/after in words)
6. Use ANALOGY — compare the mechanism to something universally understood
7. Show the SAVINGS of using the product (time, money, effort, pain)
8. Show the reader SHOWING OFF the result to others
9. Show the ABSENCE of the problem (what life is like without it)
10. DRAMATIZE one specific moment of relief — the first morning they wake up without pain
11. Use TESTIMONIAL as intensification — not social proof, but vivid description of someone's result
12. REINFORCE the desire by combining two emotional images together
13. Weaponize your GUARANTEE as a final summary of benefits — what you're promising they'll feel

### Technique 2: Identification (Building a Saleable Personality)
Build a prestige role INTO the product so it acts as a status-definer for the buyer.

**If the product has a positive primary image:** Emphasize and expand the role. "The kit serious protectors trust."

**If the product has a neutral/negative primary image:** Use that image as a "believability bridge" — acknowledge it, then gradually weave in more favorable characteristics.
- Example: "Yes, it costs more than a drugstore kit. But the people buying it are the ones who've opened a cheap kit in an emergency and found nothing useful inside."

**Character Roles** (traits your buyer wants to embody): "prepared," "responsible," "capable," "ahead of the curve," "protective"
**Achievement Roles** (status they want to signal): "the one who stays calm in a crisis," "the person my family turns to when it matters"

*[Already documented in Belief-Building section — this is Schwartz's same system]*
Start with accepted facts → build habit of agreement → lead to the conclusion they'd have initially rejected. Use: "therefore," "because," "the reason why," inclusion questions, symptom identification.

### Technique 5: Mechanization (Proving the Claims)
You must logically demonstrate HOW the product works so the prospect believes the promise — not just THAT it works.

**If the mechanism is well-known:** Name it and move on. "Color-coded compartments let you find wound care supplies in under 10 seconds."
**If the mechanism is NOT well-known:** Describe it using heavily emotionalized "selling language" — turn the mechanism into the hero of the story.
- The mechanism gets a NAME (Kit Chaos, The Panic Gap, Organization Under Pressure)
- The mechanism gets a METAPHOR (like a fire station vs. a junk drawer)
- The mechanism gets VILLAIN STATUS (it's been failing you every time it mattered)

**Rule:** Never explain a mechanism without making the reader FEEL why the mechanism matters. Science without stakes = lost readers.

### Technique 7: Momentum (Drawing the Reader Deeper)
The reader must be pulled through the copy — never allowed to stop and decide whether to continue. Every section must end with a reason to read the next.

**Momentum techniques:**
- **Open Loop transition:** End the section with an unanswered question ("But here's what makes this one different from anything you've seen before...")
- **Incomplete tease:** "I'll show you exactly how this works in a moment — but first, you need to understand WHY nothing else has worked until now."
- **The And/But/So chain:** "And that's not all... But there's something even more significant... So what does this mean for you?"
- **Peter Kell velocity words:** "And the craziest thing is..." / "But here's where it gets interesting..." / "Now here's the part most people miss..."

**Rule:** Never end a section with a period of completeness. Always leave one loop open. The reader who stops is the reader who doesn't buy.

## PART 2: POWER SENTENCE MECHANICS



### The Sentence Structure Rules

**Short sentences do heavy lifting.** Long sentences carry nuance. Use both.
- Average sentence: 10-15 words for body copy
- Stress point sentences: 3-7 words
- Complex mechanism explanations: up to 25 words, then break
- Headlines: 8-12 words ideal

**Fragments for emphasis. Like this. They signal importance.**
A fragment preceded and followed by complete sentences creates a focal point. The eye stops. The brain registers emphasis.

**Specific over general. Always.**
- ✗ "expensive" → ✓ "$127/month"
- ✗ "thousands" → ✓ "31,482 people"
- ✗ "quickly" → ✓ "in 72 hours"
- ✗ "significantly better" → ✓ "found wound closure in 8 seconds vs. 2+ minutes"
- ✗ "one night" → ✓ "2am on a Tuesday"

**Benefits lead. Features justify. Never reverse.**
- ✗ "Contains 250 components in 4 compartments" (feature first)
- ✓ "Find what you need in under 10 seconds — organized across 4 color-coded compartments with 250 components" (benefit first, feature justifies)

**"You" not "We." Always.**
- ✗ "We offer a 90-day guarantee"
- ✓ "Your results are guaranteed for 90 days"

### The Bencivenga IF/THEN Construction
Most powerful sentence structure in direct response:
```
"IF you [specific condition the prospect already has],
THEN [specific outcome the product delivers]."
```
"If you've got a family depending on you, this one kit could be the difference between panic and action in an emergency."
"If you've bought first aid kits that ended up as junk drawers, this is the reason why — and the fix."

### Embedded Commands (NLP)
Phrases embedded within longer sentences that bypass conscious resistance:
- "...and as you **imagine what it would feel like** to know your family is fully protected..."
- "...you may **notice yourself wanting** to learn more about..."
- "...and **picture yourself** the next time someone gets hurt, calmly reaching for exactly what you need..."
- "...as you **begin to understand** how this works..."
- "...and **go ahead and** take a moment to think about..."

## PART 2: NLP PATTERNS (Complete Set — 25 Patterns)


**Pattern 1: Future Pacing:** "Imagine your kid falls off the bike and you're right there — calm, confident, Zip Stitch in hand. What does that feel like?" Forces brain to simulate experience.

**Pattern 2: Presupposition:** "When you start using this, you'll notice..." Bypasses "if" and moves to "when."

**Pattern 3: Pacing and Leading:** State undeniable facts (Pace), then introduce claim (Lead). "You've bought first aid kits before. You've tossed them in a closet. And when something happened, you couldn't find what you needed. That's because..."

**Pattern 4: Cause-Effect:** "[Action] will [result] so that [benefit]." Links product to outcomes through implied causation.

**Pattern 5: Reframing:** "It's not that you don't care. It's not that you're irresponsible. It's that nobody taught you what actually belongs in an emergency kit." Absolves guilt, opens new opportunity.

**Pattern 6: Embedded Commands:** Commands hidden in larger sentences. "You might want to TRY THIS TODAY before this offer expires."

**Pattern 7: The Bind:** "Whether you choose the Large Kit for home or the Travel Kit for the car, your family is covered." All choices lead to your outcome.

**Pattern 8: The Not/Because Pattern:** "It's not expensive because you're replacing the 5 cheap kits you'd burn through in the same time — plus the ER visit you'd avoid." Negates objection while installing new belief.

**Pattern 9: Sensory Language:** "Feel the weight of the kit in your hands. Notice the click of each compartment opening. Sense the confidence building as you see everything labeled, organized, ready."

**Pattern 10: The Meta-Model Violation:** "Experience the transformation. Feel the change. Know the difference." Vague language forces reader to fill in personal meaning.

**Pattern 11: The Common Enemy Rally:** Name the external villain your prospect AND you share, then unite against it. "The first aid industry profits from selling you 50 band-aids and calling it a 'complete kit.' That's why no one teaches you what actually belongs in one."

**Pattern 12: Micro-Commitment Staircase:** Get a tiny yes → build to bigger yes → product purchase is the next natural step. Free quiz (tiny yes) → email capture (small yes) → personalized plan (medium yes) → purchase (target yes).

**Pattern 13: Hypnotic Loop (Zeigarnik Effect):** Open multiple loops simultaneously, close none until late in the copy. "I'll show you the three things that changed everything. But first — here's what my doctor said that shocked me most. [Story] Now I can tell you the third thing."

**Pattern 14: Shadow Self Confrontation:** Expose the gap between who they WANT to be and who they're CURRENTLY being. "You already know your family isn't prepared. The question isn't whether this kit works — it's whether you're willing to be the person who actually does something before the next emergency."

**Pattern 15: Identity Frame Stacking:** Build the buyer's self-image layer by layer until NOT buying contradicts their identity. "You're the kind of person who checks the smoke detectors... who keeps the car maintained... who knows that waiting to prepare for an emergency means gambling with the people you love."

**Pattern 16: Fractionation (Emotional Oscillation):** Bounce prospect rapidly between high positive and high negative emotion. Hope spike → fear anchor → hope spike (bigger) → fear anchor (specific) → hope resolution → CTA.

**Pattern 17: The Conspiracy Pivot:** "So whose fault is this, really? It's not yours. Here's what's actually happening... [Name the external system responsible]. You didn't fail at being prepared. The first aid industry failed you by selling marketing instead of medicine."

**Pattern 18: Transformation Timeline:** "Day 1: you mount the kit and feel the weight of real preparation. Day 7: your family knows exactly where it is. Day 14: you run a practice scenario and realize you're actually ready. Day 30: your neighbor asks where you got it after you helped their kid at a cookout."

**Pattern 19: The Crossroads Close:** Present three explicit paths and make only one of them sensible. Path 1: do nothing → consequences. Path 2: continue alternatives → why they fail. Path 3: this solution, risk-free → transformed life. The button IS Path 3.

**Pattern 20: The Skeptic Overcome:** State bold claim → immediately drop irrefutable proof BEFORE they can object. Never let a major claim sit unsubstantiated for more than 2-3 sentences.

**Pattern 21: Lethal Specificity:** Use hyper-specific details too precise to be fabricated. "At 11:47 AM on a Tuesday in November, sitting in my car in the Whole Foods parking lot..." The brain trusts specific over general because false memories are vague.

**Pattern 22: Identifying Victim Effect:** Build proof around ONE specific person's story. "Dave, 38, a dad from Colorado, used the Zip Stitch on his daughter's chin at a trailhead and emailed us the next day..." The brain connects to individuals, not aggregates.

**Pattern 23: Licensing Effect (You Deserve This):** Grant the prospect permission to buy based on sacrifice/struggle. "You've worked hard. You've tried harder than most people would. You've earned the right to have this work for you."

**Pattern 24: Rhyme-as-Reason:** Rhyming phrases feel truer than non-rhyming phrases. "Prepared today, protected always." / "Right kit, right time, right call." Best for: taglines, benefit statements, closing lines.

**Pattern 25: Peak-End Rule Close:** VSLs and long-form pages are judged by their emotional peak AND their ending — not the average of all content. Engineer: one extreme emotional peak (worst/darkest moment), and the last 3 sentences before the CTA as the most inspiring, vivid, and specific of the entire piece.

## PART 2C: BELIEF-BUILDING TECHNIQUES

**GRADUALIZATION (The Yes-Train):**
Start with facts the prospect already accepts. Lead them through a succession of progressively more remote claims — each prepared by the one before it.

"You know that uneasy feeling when someone gets hurt and you realize you don't have the right supplies? [YES] And you've probably noticed those cheap first aid kits are just band-aids and alcohol wipes? [YES] What most people don't realize is that 90% of first aid kits on Amazon are assembled by marketers, not medical professionals. [HMMMM] They're designed to look impressive on the listing page, not to work in an actual emergency. [THAT EXPLAINS IT] There's one kit — curated by an active-duty EMT-Paramedic — that's organized the way a real jump bag is organized. [TELL ME MORE]"

Each statement is prepared by the one before it. The big claim is acceptable only BECAUSE of the groundwork.

**REDEFINITION (Remove Objections Before They Form):**
Give a new definition to your product or its category BEFORE the objection arises.

- **Simplification:** "One kit. Mount it on the wall. You're done."
- **Escalation:** "This isn't just for cuts. Burns, sprains, allergic reactions, wound closure, trauma — one kit covers every scenario your family is likely to face."
- **Price Reduction:** "That's $0.48 per component. Less than a single box of band-aids. For a complete emergency medical system."

**INTERWEAVING (Advanced Technique):**
Every paragraph should contain desire, mechanism, and conviction woven together. Don't separate persuasion into isolated sections.

"The color-coded compartment system [mechanism] puts wound care, burn treatment, and trauma supplies exactly where your hands go [image] — eliminating the frantic searching [mechanism] that wastes critical seconds in an emergency [fear/desire], giving you the same organized response that a trained EMT relies on [identity/belief]."

One sentence. Five persuasion layers.

**CONCENTRATION (Destroying Alternatives) — Schwartz:**
Systematically destroy every other way the prospect could satisfy their desire except through your product.

1. Name each alternative (competitor products, DIY, drugstore kits)
2. Grant a small benefit: "Yes, a basic drugstore kit will handle a paper cut..."
3. Reveal the critical flaw for THIS specific problem: "...but when someone is actually bleeding, burned, or in shock, band-aids and antiseptic wipes aren't going to cut it."
4. Show the damage the inferior solution has caused or will cause
5. Introduce your product as the ONLY mechanism addressing the root problem

**Rule:** Never attack competitors by name. Attack the MECHANISM's inadequacy. Let the prospect conclude the competitor is inadequate themselves.

**CAMOUFLAGE (Borrowed Conviction) — Schwartz:**
Adopt the format, tone, and voice of the medium you're advertising in so the prospect reads your copy as editorial truth rather than advertisement.

| Medium | Camouflage Look | Voice Shift |
|---|---|---|
| Health magazine | Publication masthead, author byline, date, pull quotes | Journalistic — "Health experts are now recommending..." |
| News site | Breaking news headline, "Reports say..." | News anchor — present tense, neutral |
| Doctor blog | Clinical tone, anatomical terms, study citations, "In my practice..." | Academic-accessible |
| Reddit / forum | Lowercase, conversational, "not an ad", "just wanted to share" | Peer-to-peer |

**Rule:** The prospect should be 3-5 sentences in before their brain processes "this is an ad." By then, emotional investment is established.

## PART 2E: OFFER ARCHITECTURE CHECKLIST

Before writing copy, verify the offer is strong enough to convert. Weak offer = weak copy regardless of quality.

- [ ] **Bleeding Neck Problem:** Does the product solve an urgent, painful problem? Not a nice-to-have — a MUST-solve.
- [ ] **5-Word Answer:** Can you state what you're really selling in 5 words? If not, offer isn't clear enough.
- [ ] **Value Stack:** Core product + bonuses (speed bonus, ease bonus, expansion bonus, support bonus). Each bonus removes an objection or accelerates results.
- [ ] **Pricing Psychology:** Charm prices ($49 not $50). 3-tier structure with "Best Value" highlighted. Per-unit savings visible on bulk options.
- [ ] **Price Anchor:** What's the expensive alternative? Private coaching, doctor visits, competitor stacks — establish anchor before revealing price.
- [ ] **Risk Reversal (3-Level Hierarchy):**
  - **Level 1 — Standard Guarantee:** 30/60/90-day money-back guarantee. Essential but expected. Table stakes.
  - **Level 2 — Better-Than-Risk-Free:** "Keep the bonuses even if you return the product." "Double your money back if [specific result not achieved]." Removes risk AND adds upside.
  - **Level 3 — Named Guarantees:** Create memorable guarantee names: "The Iron-Clad Promise," "The Triple-Protection Pledge." Named guarantees feel more real and are more memorable than generic refund policies.
- [ ] **Authentic Urgency & Scarcity:**
  - *Authentic Scarcity:* Limited inventory (with reason), limited availability of expert time, limited production capacity
  - *Authentic Urgency:* Pricing tied to specific event, deadline with logical reason, phase-out of current offer
  - **WARNING:** Manufactured urgency destroys trust. Always tie urgency to logical business reason.
- [ ] **Bundle Push:** Incentivize 3-6 unit purchases. Increases AOV AND ensures customer uses product long enough to see results.
- [ ] **Monkey's Fist:** Is the first step tiny enough? "Just try one bottle" with full guarantee = lowest possible commitment.

### Offer Creation SOPs (From Titan Copy 110, Book V)

An offer is not just a product — it is a bundle of value and risk reversal. Build in this order:

**1. The Core:** Phenomenal product that solves a burning pain (Bleeding Neck problem). If the product doesn't solve a real, urgent problem, no copy can save it.

**2. The Premiums (Bonuses):**
- **Digital:** eBooks (e.g., "[Number] Natural Remedies"), Video Courses ("[Timeframe] Challenge")
- **Community:** VIP [Platform] Group access. Satisfies the Affiliation evolutionary driver.
- **Cost:** Low cost to replicate, high perceived value ($[Amount]-$[Amount] value)
- **Bonus Stacking Pattern:** Bonus 1: Speed bonus (get results faster) → Bonus 2: Ease bonus (make implementation easier) → Bonus 3: Expansion bonus (extend benefits to other areas) → Bonus 4: Support bonus (reduce risk of failure)
- **Each bonus should:** Solve a related problem, Remove an objection, Accelerate results, Add perceived value beyond cost

**3. The Economics:**
- **Bundling:** Incentivize 3-4 unit purchases. Increases AOV and ensures the customer uses the product long enough to see results.
- **Pricing Psychology:** Use charm prices ($49, not $50). Use Value Stacking (Total Value: $[High], Your Price: $[Low]).

**4. The Guarantee:** Results or Refund. Must be long enough (90-180 days) to remove all perceived risk. See Risk Reversal Hierarchy above.

## PART 3: COPY KILLERS CHECKLIST

Eliminate every one of these before any copy is approved:

| # | Killer | Why It Kills | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Vague claims** | No specificity = no believability | Replace every "many," "some," "often," "usually" with specific numbers, timeframes, names |
| 2 | **Feature-first writing** | Reptilian brain filters out features | Lead with the transformation, then explain the mechanism |
| 3 | **Passive voice** | Drains energy from copy | Make the subject act. "Is experienced by users" → "You'll feel" |
| 4 | **Fake urgency** | Destroys trust when discovered | Only use urgency tied to real business constraints with plausible reasons |
| 5 | **Adverb overload** | Signals weak writing | Kill "very," "really," "extremely," "incredibly." Strengthen the verb/noun instead. |
| 6 | **Hedging language** | Signals doubt in your own product | Remove "may," "might," "could possibly," "some users report" from core claims. Put those in disclaimers. |
| 7 | **Generic social proof** | "Thousands of customers" triggers skepticism | Always: number + "+" + specific context. "18,447+ verified buyers" |
| 8 | **Burying the offer** | Reader exits before reaching CTA | Tease the offer in the lead. State key terms (price/guarantee) early. |
| 9 | **Wall of text** | Visual deterrent. Reader doesn't even start. | Max 3 sentences per paragraph in body copy. Use white space aggressively. |
| 10 | **"We" copy** | Self-focused, not reader-focused | Audit ratio: "You/Your" should appear 3-5x more than "We/Our" |

## PART 4: THE SCHWARTZ STRENGTHENERS — Applied to Body Copy

After any draft section, run it through these to strengthen each paragraph:

**1. The Intensification Pass**
For each benefit claimed — can you make it more specific? More sensory? More time-based?
"Better prepared" → "The kind of prepared where you can treat a deep cut, a burn, and an allergic reaction without calling 911"

**2. The Antagonist Pass**
Have you named what DOESN'T work and WHY? Contrast sharpens desire.
"SurviveX works because..." needs a foil: "...unlike cheap kits that give you 200 items with no organization, no labels, and no way to find anything under pressure."

**3. The Proof Pass**
Every claim needs one of: stat, testimonial snippet, expert reference, or logical explanation.
Unsupported claim: "Everything you need in an emergency."
Supported: "Everything you need in an emergency — curated by Chase Carter, EMT-Paramedic, based on 10,000+ field hours of real-world response."

**4. The Specificity Pass**
Every time copy says vague → replace with specific.
"A long time" → "11 years"
"A big improvement" → "Found wound closure in 8 seconds vs. 2+ minutes with a standard kit"

**5. The Future-Pacing Pass**
Have you helped them FEEL the outcome? Mechanism explains HOW. Future pacing makes them WANT it.
Add one present-tense sensory visualization at every payoff moment.

### Lens 1 — Schwartz Lens

### Lens 3 — Neurochemical Lens

- Which applicable biases are missing and should be added?

### Lens 5 — Voice Lens

- Is every "we" replaced with "you" or "your"?
- Does it sound like one specific person talking to one specific person?

### Lens 6 — Compliance Lens
- Zero Tier 1 triggers (disease claims)?
- All Tier 2 terms shielded (see titan-words-forbidden.md)?
- No "YOU + Negative" framing?
- All aggressive claims inside testimonials?
- Disclaimers present where required?

### Lens 7 — Structure Lens
- Is the format's structural DNA intact (no block imports from other formats)?
- Does the copy follow the correct conversion arc for its format?
- Are there no orphaned sections that belong to a different format?

### Lens 8 — Proof Lens
- Every claim supported by: stat, testimonial, expert reference, or logical mechanism?

### Lens 9 — Offer Lens
- Is the CTA clear and action-specific?
- Is risk reversal (guarantee) explicit?
- Are urgency elements tied to real constraints?

### Lens 10 — Pattern-Interrupt Lens
- Does the hook hit at least 2 of: Contrast, Novelty, Relevance?
- Would a person scrolling on autopilot stop at this headline?
- Does each section break have a compelling reason to continue?
