# TITAN HOOK LIBRARY
## Built from analysis of 6,812 DTC ads across 84 verified brands (v5 dataset — health, safety, wellness, beauty, fitness)

> A hook has ONE job: stop the scroll and create a reason to keep reading/watching.
> The best hooks work on ONE of three levers: CURIOSITY, FEAR, or DESIRE.
> Every hook type below maps to one or more of these levers.

---

## HOOK TAXONOMY

---

### HOOK TYPE 1: CONTRARIAN / PATTERN INTERRUPT
**Lever:** Curiosity + Desire
**What it does:** Contradicts what the reader assumes is the only option. Opens by rejecting the mainstream solution before the reader has a chance to tune out. Creates immediate cognitive dissonance — the brain wants to resolve the contradiction.
**Best for:** Cold traffic, Facebook/Instagram feeds, awareness-level 3-4 audiences who've bought first aid kits before

**Formula:**
`Forget [mainstream solution] — there's a [smarter/simpler/better] way to [desired outcome]`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "Forget band-aid first aid kits — here's what EMTs actually keep in their personal kits. SurviveX was designed by a paramedic who got tired of seeing families show up to emergencies with nothing but expired Band-Aids and hope."
2. "STOP relying on a $15 kit from the gas station for your family's safety. There's a reason first responders don't carry those — and it's the same reason Chase Carter designed SurviveX."
3. "The first aid kit you bought 5 years ago? It's full of expired supplies. Here's what to carry instead — designed by an EMT-P who's seen what actually matters in the field."

### HOOK TYPE 2: WEEKLY TRANSFORMATION TIMELINE (RESULT STACK)
**Lever:** Desire
**What it does:** Shows the reader exactly what they'll experience step by step, removing ambiguity about the product. The specificity of staged experience creates a mental movie and builds anticipation. Forces the reader to project themselves into the scenario.
**Best for:** Facebook/Instagram image ads, warm audiences, product-aware shoppers who've hesitated to buy

**Formula:**
`[Desired outcome] when you open the kit:
Step 1 — [specific first experience]
Step 2 — [next discovery]
Step 3 — [deeper realization]
Step 4 — [emotional outcome]`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "What happens when your SurviveX kit arrives: Day 1 — You open it and realize you've never seen a first aid kit this organized. Every compartment is labeled and color-coded. Day 2 — You check what's inside. 250 components. Israeli bandages. Zip Stitch wound closure strips. Burn treatment. Things you didn't know existed. Day 3 — You mount it in your truck, your garage, or your kitchen wall. Day 4 — You realize this is the first time you've actually felt prepared."
2. "The SurviveX experience: Unbox — 250 expert-curated components, color-coded and labeled. Explore — discover supplies you didn't know you needed (Zip Stitch, Israeli bandages, emergency blanket). Mount — MOLLE-compatible system goes anywhere: truck, wall, pack. Feel — that quiet confidence that comes from actual preparedness."

### HOOK TYPE 3: DIRECT PROBLEM CALLOUT (STRUGGLE MIRROR)
**Lever:** Fear + Curiosity
**What it does:** Opens by naming a problem the reader already has in plain language. The reader immediately self-selects — "that's me." Creates immediate relevance and stops the scroll because the ad appears to be speaking directly to their situation.
**Best for:** Cold traffic, problem-aware audiences (awareness levels 2-3), Facebook news feed

**Formula:**
`[Specific scenario or pain point]?
[1-sentence expansion of the problem]
[Consequence if unaddressed]...`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "When was the last time you actually checked what's inside your first aid kit? If it's been more than a year, there's a good chance half your supplies are expired, packaging is cracked, and the things you'd actually need in an emergency — wound closure strips, hemostatic gauze, real bandages — were never in there to begin with."
2. "Your kid falls off the bike. Blood everywhere. You grab the first aid kit and open it — and there's nothing useful inside. Just a few loose Band-Aids and a packet of aspirin from 2019. That moment of helplessness? 200,000+ families decided they'd never feel it again."
3. "Ever been in an emergency and realized you had nothing? No real bandages. No wound closure. No burn treatment. Just paper towels and panic. That's the moment SurviveX was designed for."

### HOOK TYPE 4: FIRST-PERSON PERSONAL STORY (ANTI-ANNOUNCEMENT)
**Lever:** Curiosity + Fear + Desire (all three — the most powerful combination)
**What it does:** Opens in the first person with a personal disclosure or vulnerable admission that sounds like it came from a friend's Facebook post, not an ad. The "I wasn't going to share this" construction creates intimacy and disarms skepticism.
**Best for:** Long-form Facebook ads, advertorials, story video scripts, VSL leads, cold traffic

**Formula:**
`I wasn't going to share this because it's [personal/embarrassing], but if it helps even one person [like you], it's worth it.
[Set the scene: who you are, what was happening, how bad it got]
[The moment everything changed]`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "I wasn't going to post this, but my wife insisted. Last month our daughter slipped on wet rocks at the lake. Deep cut on her shin — bone visible. I grabbed our SurviveX kit from the truck. Color-coded compartments — I found the wound closure strips in seconds. Zip Stitch held the wound edges together while we drove to urgent care. The doctor said whoever did the initial wound closure did it right. That was me. A dad with a good kit and zero medical training."
2. "I'll never forget the look on my neighbor's face when I pulled out a real first aid kit. His kid had fallen off the trampoline — possible broken arm, definitely a bad gash. He had nothing. I had everything. That's the day I stopped thinking of my SurviveX as something I 'might need someday' and started thinking of it as something I carry everywhere."

### HOOK TYPE 5: INSTITUTION / INJUSTICE HOOK (US vs. SYSTEM)
**Lever:** Fear + Curiosity + Identity
**What it does:** Opens by calling out a systemic failure — the first aid kit industry, corporate marketing, big-box retailers selling inadequate kits. Positions the reader as someone who's been underserved. Activates tribal identity and builds emotional alignment before any product mention.
**Best for:** Cold traffic targeting frustrated/skeptical audiences, long-form advertorials, preparedness communities

**Formula:**
`When [professionals] need first aid supplies, they get [professional-grade equipment].
When [YOUR avatar] needs first aid supplies, they get [what the industry gives them].
[Setup the injustice. Make it personal. Make it specific.]`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "When an EMT needs wound closure in the field, they reach for medical-grade strips designed to hold tissue together under pressure. When a parent needs wound closure at home? They get a box of Band-Aids that won't stick to wet skin. The first aid kit industry has been selling you the bare minimum for decades — and charging you like it's professional gear. Chase Carter spent 10 years as a paramedic before he decided to build the kit he wished every family had."
2. "The first aid kit industry doesn't want you to know this: most 'complete' kits sold at big-box stores contain 80% filler — tiny alcohol swabs, single-use items that expire in 6 months, and zero real trauma supplies. They cost $30 and make you feel prepared. You're not. SurviveX was designed by an EMT who got tired of showing up to emergencies where people had kits but no real supplies."

**Lever:** Curiosity + Fear

`What's actually wrong with [common solution]?
[Unexpected root cause or hidden problem].
That's why [product] was designed differently — [specific difference]`

### HOOK TYPE 8: OFFER-FIRST / DISCOUNT HOOK

**Best for:** Retargeting, warm audiences, seasonal campaigns, existing customers, promotional periods

**Formula:**
`Free Shipping + [Offer] — Ends [Date]`

### HOOK TYPE 16: CURIOSITY QUESTION HOOK
**Lever:** Curiosity
**What it does:** Opens with a question that forces the reader to immediately form an answer — and then wonder if their answer is wrong. Exploits the Zeigarnik effect.
**Best for:** Cold traffic, Facebook/Instagram ads, story video openings

**Formula:**
`What [can/would/should] someone do when [emergency scenario]?
[Surprising answer or unexpected insight]`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "What would you actually do if someone in your family was bleeding badly right now? Not what you think you'd do. What you'd actually do. Most people freeze. Not because they're weak — because they don't have the right supplies within reach. SurviveX fixes that."
2. "What's the one component EMTs carry that no consumer first aid kit includes? Wound closure strips. Specifically, strips designed to hold wound edges together until you can get to a doctor. SurviveX includes Zip Stitch — the component that changes everything about a first aid kit."
3. "Quick: where is your first aid kit right now? Can you picture it? When was the last time you opened it? If you hesitated on any of those questions, you're like 90% of families. SurviveX was designed for exactly this moment."

## HOOK SELECTION GUIDE

| Traffic Temperature | Best Hook Types |
|---|---|
| Cold (no awareness) | Contrarian/Pattern Interrupt, Direct Problem Callout, Science Myth-Bust, Curiosity Question, Warning/Stop-Scroll, **Humor/Fun** |
| Warm (problem aware) | Personal Story, Institution/Injustice, Avatar Callout, Comparison/Us vs. Them, Personal Test/Experiment, Authority Discovery |
| Hot (solution aware) | Offer-First/Discount, Benefit Stack, Unboxing Timeline, Social Proof Scale, Winback |
| Retargeting | Offer-First, Winback, Social Proof Scale, Quote Lead/Testimonial-First, **Humor/Fun** |

**Format note:** Personal Story, Group Mirror, Institution/Injustice, and Avatar Callout are primarily effective in video and long-form advertorial. In static image ads, the top performers are Benefit Stack, Facts/Stats, Discount, and Testimonial.

## COMBINATIONS THAT PERFORM BEST

**Key insight from v5 data:** FOTW brands use 25% more hook types per ad than average brands. They LAYER hooks — combining benefits + facts + testimonials in a single ad rather than relying on one hook type. When writing for SurviveX, default to multi-hook construction.

Based on patterns in the dataset, these hook pairings in title + body work consistently:

1. **Title: Offer-First + Body: Benefit Stack**
   `"Free Shipping $75+ — This Week" + "250 components. Color-coded. EMT-designed. Zip Stitch wound closure. $120.99."`

2. **Title: Curiosity Question + Body: Science Myth-Bust**
   `"What's Actually Inside Your First Aid Kit?" + "Most kits are 80% filler...here's what an EMT actually carries..."`

3. **Title: Avatar Callout + Body: Personal Story + Social Proof**
   `"I'm Done With Cheap First Aid Kits..." + first-person story of being unprepared + "200,000+ kits sold"`

4. **Title: Warning + Body: Comparison + Personal Test**
   `"Read Before You Buy Another First Aid Kit" + "I compared 6 kits side by side..."`

5. **Title: Offer + Body: Benefit Stack + Quote Lead**
   `"FSA/HSA Eligible — Use Your Benefits" + component list + customer testimonial`

- The product has a strong "expert curation" story — designed by Chase Carter, EMT-P. This makes hooks 7 (Science Myth-Bust), 12 (Discovery), and 19 (Authority Discovery) especially powerful.
- The "everyone has a kit, nobody has the right kit" angle maps directly to hook type 1 (Contrarian) and hook type 17 (Warning).
- Avatar is "The Protector" — responsible, 28-45, provider. Hooks 5 (Institution/Injustice), 11 (Avatar Callout), and 3 (Direct Problem Callout) speak to the "I refuse to be helpless" identity.
- The Zip Stitch wound closure is a unique differentiator — use in Benefit Stack (10), Comparison (14), and Discovery (12) hooks.
- Color-coded organization is visually demonstrable — strong for video hooks and image ads.
- FSA/HSA eligibility and free shipping $75+ are strong Offer-First (8) elements.

**For SurviveX Specialty Products (Zip Stitch, Israeli Bandages, Burn Kit, Travel Kit):**
- Personal Test (type 9) works well — "I tested every wound closure strip on the market."
- Benefit Stack (type 10) shines for component-rich products.
- Curiosity Question (type 16) opens well: "What do EMTs carry that most people have never heard of?"

**High-performance hook length by format:**
- Facebook image ad body: 50-150 words with hook in first sentence
- Facebook long-form / advertorial ad: 500-2,500 words — hook must hold through the first paragraph
- Instagram story video: Hook must land in 0-3 seconds (visual + text overlay)
- VSL script: Hook sentence 1 = either problem callout or contrarian statement

## HOOKS 18-20 — DISCOVERED FROM 3,849 VIDEO AD ANALYSIS (2026-03-06)

### HOOK TYPE 18: The Group Mirror
**Lever:** Identity / Belonging
**v5 frequency:** 0.6% of ads, 4 brands (niche)
**What it does:** Viewer self-selects into a collective "we" without being directly called out. No "YOU" — softer than Avatar Callout, bypasses defensiveness.
**Best for:** Broad cold audiences, parents, family-oriented demographics

**Formula:**
`"Some of us [specific behavior A]. Others of us [different version of same pain]. We're all [shared frustration with current solutions]. We all want [shared desire]."`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "Some of us have a first aid kit we bought 5 years ago and never opened. Others have one that's missing half its supplies. We've all had that moment — someone gets hurt and we realize we have nothing useful. We all want the same thing: to be the person who's actually ready."
2. "Some of us keep a first aid kit in the car. Others keep one in the house. Most of us have never checked what's actually inside either one. We all want to believe we're prepared. SurviveX is for the ones who want to actually BE prepared."

---

### HOOK TYPE 19: Authority Discovery / "What If I Claimed..."
**Lever:** Curiosity + Authority
**v5 frequency:** 11.2% of ads, 35 brands (widely adopted — 7th most common hook type)
**What it does:** Opens with a rhetorical question that invites the viewer in, then delivers a softened but real claim, then introduces the authority figure AFTER curiosity is established.
**Best for:** Cold traffic, skeptical audiences, preparedness/tactical communities

**Formula:**
`"What would you say if I claimed [extraordinary thing]? Well, [soften slightly], but [real version of the claim is still remarkable]. I'm [Authority Name/Title] and what I discovered..."`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "What would you say if I told you that the first aid kit you're relying on right now is probably 60% filler and 30% expired? You'd probably say I'm exaggerating. But I'm Chase Carter — I've been a paramedic for 10+ years. I've responded to thousands of calls where people had first aid kits. Almost none of them had the supplies that actually mattered. That's why I designed SurviveX."
2. "What if I told you that the difference between panicking and responding calmly in an emergency often comes down to one thing — organization? Not training. Not experience. Organization. I'm an EMT-P, and after 10 years in the field, I can tell you: the person with the right supplies, clearly labeled, always performs better than the person with a medical degree and a jumbled bag."

---

### HOOK TYPE 20: Regret Prevention / "What If?"
**Lever:** Fear (of inaction) + Identity
**v5 frequency:** 1.8% of ads, 12 brands
**What it does:** Flips standard fear-based hooks. Instead of fearing the problem, the viewer fears their own future inaction.
**Best for:** Warm audiences, retargeting, decision-stage prospects

**Formula:**
`"[Name] took action because [they] didn't want to [face a scenario unprepared]. As [Name] said: 'You WILL regret [inaction]. You won't regret [taking action].'" OR direct: "Don't wait until [emergency] — [time from now] will come regardless."`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "Marcus bought his SurviveX kit in May. In August, his daughter fell at the park — deep cut, lots of blood. He had everything he needed. As he put it: 'I can't imagine what that day would have looked like without this kit. I would have been standing there with nothing.' Don't wait for the emergency to wish you'd been prepared."
2. "Every family that orders a SurviveX kit says the same thing: 'I wish I'd bought this sooner.' Not because they needed it yet. Because the peace of mind started the day it arrived. Don't wait for the emergency. Be ready before it happens."

---

### HOOK TYPE 21: Humor / Fun / Relatability
**Lever:** Pattern Interrupt + Desire (via positive emotion)
**v5 frequency:** 7.7% of ads, 34 brands (9th most common)
**What it does:** Uses humor, memes, or relatable everyday scenarios to stop the scroll through positive emotion. Humor ads have the highest shareability.
**Best for:** Cold traffic, broad audiences, brand awareness, lifestyle-oriented content

**Formula:**
`[Relatable scenario everyone has experienced] + [Humorous twist that connects to the product]`

**SurviveX examples:**
1. "Your first aid kit: 47 tiny Band-Aids, a packet of aspirin from 2019, and one antiseptic wipe that's been open for 6 months. Their first aid kit: 250 components, color-coded, designed by an actual paramedic, Zip Stitch wound closure strips. Upgrade the kit. SurviveX."
2. "Me explaining to my wife why I need ANOTHER first aid kit: 'This one is for the truck. The other one is for the house. The small one is for hiking. This is a SYSTEM, Karen.' SurviveX. For the person who takes preparedness personally."
3. "POV: Your buddy asks for a Band-Aid and you pull out a full EMT-grade first aid system with color-coded compartments and Israeli bandages. 'Bro, I just have a paper cut.' SurviveX. Maybe a little overkill. Maybe exactly right."

## VIDEO FORMAT INSIGHT (from 3,849 winning video ads)

**99.5% of winning Facebook/Instagram video ads are under 1 minute.**

This changes how to think about video copy:
- The 3-minute story VSL is for YouTube and long-form placements
- Facebook/Instagram winning format = 30-60 second cut
- Every 3-minute master script needs a 45-second Facebook-first cut (not just a 1-minute cut)
- Hook must work in 0-5 seconds — the first sentence is everything

**Recommended cut hierarchy:**
1. 3-min master (YouTube, landing page video, long-form)
2. 60-sec Facebook cut (primary paid social)
3. 45-sec Instagram cut (same as 60-sec but tighter)
4. 15-sec hook-only (for retargeting audiences)
