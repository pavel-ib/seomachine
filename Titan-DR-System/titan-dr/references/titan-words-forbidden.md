# TITAN COMPLIANCE & FORBIDDEN WORDS

## THE TWO-TIER RISK SYSTEM

Two separate risk systems govern first aid product advertising. Confuse them and you either lose your account or leave money on the table.

- **FTC/FDA RISK** = Regulatory grey area. Could trigger warning letter or FTC action. But ads run TODAY.
- **META BAN RISK** = Automated bot flags. Ad rejected in minutes. Account disabled. No traffic TODAY.

**Strategy: Avoid Tier 1 (instant bans). Embrace Tier 2 (regulatory grey area) with Shields. This is where the money is made.**

## TIER 1 — DEATH ZONE (Never Use in Ads)

> **This is not optional compliance theater.** These words get ads disapproved,
> accounts banned, and brands sued. Every word on this list has a consequence.
> Know it. Avoid it. Use the alternatives instead.

## TIER 1 — NEVER USE (Absolute Claims & Medical Device Claims)

These words, when connected to a first aid product in a way that implies it replaces professional medical care, constitutes a medical device claim, or guarantees life-saving outcomes, are forbidden. Zero tolerance. No exceptions.

Meta's automated systems flag these in minutes. Repeated violations = account disabled.

| Trigger | Why It Kills |
|---------|-------------|
| "Replaces professional medical care" | Implies product substitutes for emergency services |
| "Guaranteed to save lives" | Unsubstantiated absolute claim |
| "Medical device" (unless FDA registered) | Regulatory classification violation |
| "FDA approved" (unless true) | False regulatory claim — instant flag |
| "Prevents death" | Absolute life/death claim — instant flag |
| "Treats injuries" / "Treats wounds" | Medical treatment claim |
| "Cures" / "Treats" / "Prevents disease" | Medical claim — instant flag |
| "YOU + Negative Attribute" | Personal Attributes Policy violation |
| Before/After injury transformation photos | Graphic content + implied medical claim |
| "Guaranteed results" / "Instant relief" | Unsubstantiated claim trigger |
| Fake play buttons / UI elements | Deceptive UI violation |

**Tier 1 Extended — Death Zone:**
- cure / cures / cured
- treat / treats / treatment / treating (in medical context)
- diagnose / diagnosis
- prevent / prevents / prevention (in disease/death context)
- "replaces 911" / "replaces emergency services" / "replaces a doctor"
- "guaranteed to save lives" / "will save your life"
- "medical device" / "medical equipment" (unless FDA registered)
- "FDA approved" / "FDA cleared" (unless actually true)
- "prevents death" / "stops death" / "keeps you alive"
- "treats injuries" / "treats wounds" / "treats trauma"
- "heals wounds" (body-level healing claim)
- "stops all bleeding" (absolute claim)
- "as good as a hospital" / "hospital replacement"
- "eliminates infection" / "kills bacteria" (drug claim)
- "prevents scarring" (medical outcome claim)

> **Rule:** If the sentence could appear on a prescription drug label or FDA-regulated medical device, it's a Tier 1 violation.

## THE 6 SHIELD PROTOCOLS

Required whenever using any Tier 2 term. These make the difference between compliant and non-compliant copy.

**SHIELD 1: The Qualifier Shield**
Add qualifying language to strong claims.
"Designed to help manage minor wounds*" — not "treats wounds."
"Equipped with supplies to help control bleeding*" — not "stops all bleeding."

**SHIELD 2: The Professional Care Disclaimer (Use This Exact Copy)**
> *"Not a substitute for professional medical care. For serious injuries, always call 911. This product provides first aid supplies to help manage injuries until professional help arrives."

**SHIELD 3: The Testimonial Disclaimer**
> "Results are based on individual experiences and may not reflect the typical purchaser's experience. Always seek professional medical attention for serious injuries."

**SHIELD 4: The Testimonial Loophole**
Put specific emergency scenarios INSIDE testimonials attributed to real, named customers. This shifts from brand claim to user story.
- Example: "I used my SurviveX kit when my son fell off his bike — the color-coded compartments meant I found what I needed in seconds. — Sarah M., verified buyer"
- Deploy: Named customer + specific scenario + personal experience language

**SHIELD 5: The Expert Citation Shield**
Professional credentials and expert design appear in context of citing expertise — NOT as medical claims.
- "Designed by Chase Carter, EMT-P with 10+ years of field experience"
- "Curated based on the supplies EMTs carry in their personal kits"
- Frame: "The expert designed..." not "This product guarantees..."

**SHIELD 6: The Preparedness Story Defense**
Emergency scenarios appear as PROBLEMS in personal narrative, not product guarantee claims.
- "Last year my family was hiking when my daughter slipped on a rock. I had nothing — just paper napkins and hope. That's the day I decided never to be unprepared again."
- These are personal stories about preparedness, not product performance promises.

## THE PERSONAL ATTRIBUTES TRAP (Most Common Meta Rejection)

- BANNED: "Are you unprepared for emergencies?" / "Is your family at risk?" / "You don't have proper first aid supplies"
- APPROVED: "Many families realize they're unprepared only when an emergency happens..."
- **Rule:** Shift from second-person accusation to third-person observation. Never assert the viewer IS unprepared or at risk. Describe it as something that HAPPENS to people.

**First Aid Specific Examples:**
- BANNED: "You're putting your family at risk"
- BANNED: "Your first aid kit is useless"
- BANNED: "You're not prepared for emergencies"
- APPROVED: "Most families don't realize their first aid kit is missing critical supplies..."
- APPROVED: "If someone in your family has ever needed first aid and you couldn't find the right supplies..."
- APPROVED: "Many people discover their first aid kit has expired supplies only when they need them most..."

## ADS vs. ON-PAGE COPY — SCOPE RULES

**Meta Ads:** Bot-scanned in real time. Tier 1 = instant rejection. Tier 2 = runs with shields. Strict zone.

**On-Page Copy (landing pages, listicles, advertorials):** NOT scanned the same way. More freedom. FTC still applies, but Meta's automated enforcement is focused on the ad unit.

**Our approach for on-page — conservative:**
Even with more freedom, always maintain:
- Professional care disclaimer (Shield 2)
- Qualifiers on strong claims (Shield 1)
- Specific scenarios inside named testimonials (Shield 4)
- Expert credentials cited accurately (Shield 5)
- Testimonial disclaimer present (Shield 3)

**Why not go aggressive like competitors?** FTC can issue enforcement actions for on-page claims regardless of Meta's enforcement. The shields are invisible to readers and cost nothing.

## META TECHNICAL SURVIVAL (2025+)

**The Health & Wellness Restriction:** As of January 2025, Meta restricts health/wellness brands from optimizing for "Purchase" and "Add to Cart" events. 30-40% efficiency drop reported.

**Workarounds:**
1. **Server-Side CAPI** — Send conversion data server-to-server, bypassing browser restrictions. Sanitize all health-related parameters before sending.
2. **Custom Events** — Replace "Purchase" with neutral names ("Complete_Order") via server-side implementation. NEVER rename events in browser — domain gets flagged.
3. **First-Party Data** — Collect emails/phone at checkout, upload to Meta for matching and lookalike building.
4. **Upper-Funnel Optimization** — If lower-funnel events blocked, optimize for Landing Page Views, Content Views, or Meta Instant Forms.

**Check:** Meta Events Manager → Settings → Manage Data Source Categories. If incorrectly classified as "Health and Wellness," submit review request. First aid kits may qualify for reclassification as "Safety Equipment" or "General Merchandise."

## META / FACEBOOK AD POLICY VIOLATIONS

Meta's ad review system flags specific language patterns. These will get ads rejected or accounts restricted.

### Before/After Prohibition
Meta bans images and copy that imply dramatic transformation:
- No graphic injury images (before treatment / after treatment)
- No images of severe wounds, blood, or trauma
- Acceptable: Show the organized kit, the color-coded compartments, the clean product

### Personal Attribute Targeting Language
Meta prohibits copy that implies awareness of the user's personal safety status:
- No: "You're unprepared for emergencies" (implies knowledge of their situation)
- No: "Your family is at risk" (same)
- No: "You don't have the right supplies" (too personal, too specific)
- Yes: "Many families discover they're unprepared only when it's too late..." (general framing — compliant)
- Yes: "If you've ever scrambled to find a bandage when someone was hurt..." (conditional — compliant)

### "YOU + Negative" Framing — NEVER USE
Directly telling a user something is wrong with THEM:
- No: "You are unprepared"
- No: "Your family isn't protected"
- No: "You have an inadequate first aid kit"
- Yes: "Families who prepare ahead of time respond faster in emergencies" (third person)
- Yes: "If you've ever wished you had better supplies on hand..." (conditional, non-accusatory)

### Banned Advertising Categories
These topics require special ad account permissions or are fully prohibited:
- Graphic injury content
- Fear-mongering about specific disasters
- Unsubstantiated safety claims
- Misleading urgency (fake countdowns)

### Urgency Language Restrictions
Countdown timers and urgency claims that are fake or misleading get flagged:
- No: "Offer expires in 3 hours" (evergreen — never actually expires)
- Yes: "Limited batch — only 847 kits remain from this production run" (real constraint, real reason)
- Yes: "Price increases [specific date]" (if true)
- Yes: "Free shipping over $75 — ends [date]" (if true)

- "buy now" / "order now" / "purchase today"
- "act now" / "don't miss out"
- "limited time offer"
- "special discount"
- "click here to buy"

**Replace with editorial-adjacent language:**

- "learn more" / "find out where to get it"
- "readers are reporting..."
- "you can find this at..."

## WORDS THAT KILL CREDIBILITY

These phrases signal weak, vague, or unsubstantiated writing:

| Weak Word/Phrase | Why It Kills | Strong Alternative |
|-----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| "very effective" | Subjective, unspecific | "250 components organized into color-coded compartments" |
| "amazing results" | Marketing-speak | Specific result: "found the right bandage in under 10 seconds" |
| "powerful kit" | Meaningless | Name the specifics: "250 components including Zip Stitch, Israeli bandages, and burn treatment" |
| "high-quality materials" | Overused, no meaning | Specific: "900D polyester exterior, IPX7 waterproof rating" |
| "thousands of customers" | Vague | "200,000+ kits sold since launch" |
| "many people say" | Lacks credibility | "Based on 4,200+ verified customer reviews..." |
| "may help" (as a hedge) | Signals lack of confidence | Reframe: "Designed to help manage minor wounds*" |
| "clinically proven" | Overused, scrutinized | "Curated by Chase Carter, EMT-P, based on 10+ years of field experience" |
| "miracle" | Banned connotation, also Meta violation | Name what actually happens |
| "secret" (overused) | Lost impact | "The overlooked reason why most kits fail in real emergencies..." |
| "guaranteed to save your life" | Implies warranty on outcomes | "Backed by our 30-day satisfaction guarantee" |

## THE TITAN ZONE — 5 COPY TECHNIQUES

The "Titan Zone" is the precise space where claims are strong enough to convert but structured to survive automated review AND regulatory scrutiny.

**The Bot vs. Human Paradox:** You are always writing for two audiences simultaneously:
- **The Bot (Meta AI):** Scans for keywords, phrases, image patterns. Cannot understand implied meaning. Flags patterns, not intent.
- **The Human (Consumer):** Responds to context, emotion, implied meaning, preparedness stories.

**Core Principle:** Write VANILLA for the Bot (avoid trigger patterns) while writing SPICY for the Human (convey benefits through subtext, story, and implication).

---

### Technique 1: The User Story Defense

First-person testimonial language shifts claims from objective facts to subjective experiences. Subjective experiences cannot be proven false — they are personal accounts.

| Bad: Objective Claim (Risky) | Good: Subjective Experience (Safer) |
|---------------------------|----------------------------------|
| This kit saves lives | "Having this kit in my truck gave me the confidence to help when a car accident happened in front of me" |
| Stops bleeding instantly | "I grabbed the Israeli bandage and within seconds I felt in control of the situation" |
| Replaces the emergency room | "For the first time, I didn't feel helpless waiting for the ambulance" |
| Professional medical equipment | "The supplies felt like what I've seen EMTs use on the job" |
| Prevents wound infection | "The wound stayed clean and I felt like I'd done the right thing" |

---

### Technique 2: The Mechanism Focus

Instead of claiming specific outcomes, explain HOW the product works at a design level. This shifts attention from guaranteed results to engineering and expertise — which is both more credible and more compliant.

| Bad: Outcome Claim (Risky) | Good: Mechanism Focus (Safer) |
|-------------------------|---------------------------|
| Saves lives in emergencies | Color-coded compartments eliminate panic and put the right supplies in your hands in seconds |
| Stops bleeding | Zip Stitch strips use adhesive wound closure technology to hold wound edges together |
| Professional medical care | Curated by Chase Carter, EMT-P — every component selected based on real-world field use |
| Prevents infection | Individually sealed antiseptic supplies maintain sterility until the moment you need them |

**The Design Story:** Focus on what the engineering does at a functional level:
- "Color-coded labeled compartments mean you find what you need without thinking — even under pressure*"
- "900D polyester exterior built to survive the same conditions you carry it into"

---

### Technique 3: The "What If" Frame

Rhetorical questions plant ideas without making statements. The question format implies possibility without asserting fact — legally and mechanically different from a direct claim.

| Bad: Statement (Assertive) | Good: Question (Implied) |
|-------------------------|----------------------|
| This kit will save your family | What if you had exactly the right supplies when your family needed them most? |
| You'll handle any emergency | Imagine knowing you could confidently respond to any injury — instead of freezing |
| Your current kit is useless | What if your current first aid kit is missing the supplies that matter most? |
| You need this | What would it feel like to know you're truly prepared? |

**Imagination triggers:** Words like "imagine," "picture," and "what if" activate the reader's own imagination. They make the claim — you don't.

---

### Technique 4: The Qualifier Arsenal

Qualifying language converts absolute claims into defensible statements. Stack qualifiers for maximum protection.

| Bad: Absolute (Risky) | Good: Qualified (Safer) |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Saves lives | Designed to help save lives |
| Stops all bleeding | Includes supplies to help control bleeding |
| Professional medical care | Built to professional first responder standards |
| Guaranteed survival | Designed for maximum preparedness |
| Always works | 200,000+ customers trust SurviveX |

**Qualifier stacking:** "Designed with expert-curated supplies that may help you respond confidently to common injuries until professional help arrives.*" — three qualifiers in one sentence.

---

### Technique 5: Puffery vs. Fact

Puffery is exaggerated, subjective claims no reasonable consumer would take literally. It is legally protected as opinion — not a factual assertion requiring substantiation.

| Good: Puffery (Legal Opinion) | Bad: Factual Claim (Must Substantiate) |
|---------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| The best first aid kit on the market | Clinically proven to reduce infection rates |
| You'll feel unstoppable | Stops bleeding 40% faster than competitors |
| Life-changing preparedness | Saves 95% of trauma victims |
| The only kit you'll ever need | FDA-approved medical device |

**Rule:** If a reasonable consumer would interpret it as an objective, measurable claim — it's a fact that needs substantiation. If they'd read it as enthusiastic opinion — it's puffery.

## STRUCTURAL PROTOCOL: SURVIVEX 12-BLOCK PAGE ARCHITECTURE

> Adapted from the proven 12-Block direct sales page structure for first aid products.

**The 12-Block Structure:**

| Block | Section | Purpose |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 1 | **Hero** | Headline + hero image of organized kit |
| 2 | **Hook** | "HERE'S WHY..." — the preparedness problem |
| 3 | **Origin** | Chase Carter EMT-P story — why he designed this |
| 4 | **Problem** | The reality of cheap/expired/disorganized first aid kits |
| 5 | **Mechanism** | Color-coded organization, Zip Stitch technology, expert curation |
| 6 | **Product Range** | Large Kit → Large Pro → Waterproof → Small/Travel → Specialty |
| 7 | **Comparison** | SurviveX vs. competitors (MyMedic, NAR, Everlit, generic kits) |
| 8 | **Founder** | Chase Carter's personal story — field experience + design philosophy |
| 9 | **Offer** | Pricing, free shipping $75+, FSA/HSA eligible, bundles |
| 10 | **Reviews** | 200,000+ kits sold, verified customer stories |
| 11 | **FAQ** | Objection handling (every FAQ answers an objection) |
| 12 | **Footer** | Disclaimer, legal (Professional Care Disclaimer — Shield 2) |

**Key Compliance Patterns in This Structure:**
- Block 4 (Problem): Emergency scenarios appear as problems people face, NOT as product guarantees
- Block 5 (Mechanism): Uses "designed to" and "helps" qualifiers consistently
- Block 8 (Founder): EMT credentials appear as design authority, not medical advice
- Block 10 (Reviews): Specific number (200,000+) not rounded — activates Specificity Bias
